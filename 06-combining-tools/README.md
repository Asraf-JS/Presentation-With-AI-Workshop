# 06 — Combining PowerPoint and Canva Seamlessly

The biggest risk when using two tools together is visual inconsistency. A deck where some slides look like one thing and others look like something else signals that no one was in charge of the whole.

## Managing Fonts, Colours, and Layouts

Visual consistency across a deck comes down to three things: fonts, colours, and spacing. These need to be locked down before you start designing anything in either tool.

### Fonts

Decide on a maximum of two typefaces: one for headings, one for body text. Write them down.

When importing Canva visuals into PowerPoint as images, fonts are baked into the PNG — so there is no substitution risk. The issue is visual match. If your PowerPoint deck uses Calibri for body text and your Canva slides use a rounded sans-serif, the pages will look like they came from different documents.

**Practical rule:** In Canva, use a font that closely matches what you are using in PowerPoint. If your PowerPoint uses Calibri, use Calibri in Canva too (it is available). If you want something more distinctive in Canva, use it only on the slides that are full Canva exports (cover, dividers) and keep the data slides in PowerPoint with standard fonts.

### Colours

Write down your hex codes before you start. You need:
- Primary colour (used for headings, key highlights)
- Secondary colour (used for accents, chart colours)
- Background colour (usually white or off-white)
- Text colour (usually dark navy or near-black — not pure black)

Apply these consistently. Every element in both tools should map to one of these four colours. If you find yourself adding a fifth colour, stop and ask whether it is necessary.

### Layouts and spacing

Margins and padding are harder to match across tools but worth the effort. In PowerPoint, look at your slide margins and approximate the content area. In Canva, match that spacing visually by leaving similar margins around your content.

A common mistake is centering text in Canva on a slide that uses left-aligned text in PowerPoint. Pick one alignment and use it everywhere.

## Avoiding Visual Inconsistency

### The five-second test

Before finalising a slide, look at it for five seconds and then look at the previous slide. Ask: do these two slides look like they belong to the same deck?

Common sources of inconsistency:

**Different heading sizes.** If your PowerPoint headings are 24pt and your Canva slides have headings that look much larger or smaller when inserted, it breaks the visual rhythm.

**Competing visual styles.** One slide has a flat, minimal design. The next has gradients and shadows. Pick one and use it throughout.

**Image inconsistency.** Some slides have photos, others have icons, others have illustrations. Mix-and-match visuals signal that no one edited the deck as a whole. Decide on one visual language.

**Mismatched table styles.** PowerPoint tables and Canva tables look different by default. If you are using both, either standardise them or keep all tables in PowerPoint.

## Workflow Best Practices Between Tools

A practical order of operations for a mixed-tool deck:

1. **Build the full deck in PowerPoint first.** Get all the content in, all slides in order, titles written, data charts done. This is your master deck.

2. **Identify which slides to redesign in Canva.** Typically 2-4 slides: cover, one key message slide, one summary slide.

3. **Build those slides in Canva.** Use your locked-down colour and font choices. Export as PNG.

4. **Insert the Canva PNGs into PowerPoint.** Delete the original PowerPoint slide content, insert the PNG, and size it to fill the slide area.

5. **Do a final consistency pass.** Go through the full deck in slideshow view. Every transition from a PowerPoint slide to a Canva-origin slide is a potential inconsistency point.

### File management

Keep your Canva designs saved in your Canva account with clear names matching your slide numbers: `NC-Q3-Review-Cover`, `NC-Q3-Review-KeyMessage`, and so on. This makes it easy to go back and update individual slides without rebuilding from scratch.
