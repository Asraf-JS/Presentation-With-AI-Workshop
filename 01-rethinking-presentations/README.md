# 01 — Rethinking Presentations in the Age of AI

Before we start improving slides, it helps to name the real problems. Most corporate decks fail for the same handful of reasons, and AI can help with some of them, but not all.

## Common Problems in Corporate Decks

Most presentation problems fall into a few predictable categories.

**Too much text.** Slides get used as documents. Every point that was supposed to be spoken ends up written out in full sentences, which means the audience reads ahead and stops listening.

**No clear message.** The deck covers a lot of ground but never answers the question the audience actually needs answered: *so what?* Information is presented without a point.

**Misused visuals.** Charts that show everything rather than one thing. Tables dropped in from Excel with no simplification. Stock images that add no meaning.

**Inconsistent design.** Four different fonts, three colour schemes, slides that look like they came from different decks. This signals low effort, even when the content is solid.

**Slide-by-slide thinking.** Each slide gets treated as its own document rather than one beat in a story. The result is a collection of slides, not a presentation.

## Where AI Fits In

AI tools can genuinely help at certain stages of presentation work. Understanding where they add value, and where they do not, saves you from over-relying on them.

| Stage | What AI can help with | What AI cannot do |
|-------|-----------------------|-------------------|
| **Ideation** | Suggest structures, outline flows, generate first drafts | Know your audience, your organisation, your politics |
| **Writing** | Rewrite dense text for clarity, trim bullet points, suggest slide headlines | Judge whether a message is strategically right |
| **Design** | Suggest layouts, match colour palettes, resize elements | It picks what is common, not what is distinctive |
| **Review** | Flag overloaded slides, identify vague headlines, suggest alternatives | Understand tone, nuance, or what your audience will actually respond to |

The practical takeaway: AI accelerates the mechanical work. The thinking, what the message is, why it matters, what the audience needs, still has to come from you.

---

## Limitations to Know Upfront

**AI design suggestions tend toward the generic.** PowerPoint Designer and Canva's AI templates produce clean, acceptable results. They will rarely produce something distinctive. Use them as a starting point, not a final output.

**AI-generated text sounds like AI.** Slide text written entirely by AI tends to read as polished but hollow, correct, inoffensive, and forgettable. Use AI to improve your writing, not replace it.

**AI does not know your context.** It does not know that your CFO hates pie charts, that your department is being restructured, or that the last time this topic came up in a meeting it caused a fight. You bring that knowledge.

**Free features have real limits.** This workshop uses free or commonly available AI features in PowerPoint and Canva. The paid tiers (Copilot for Microsoft 365, Canva Pro) offer more, but you can accomplish a lot without them.

## Using AI to Diagnose Design Problems

One of the most practical things you can do with AI in this module is describe a slide and ask it to identify what is wrong. This is not about fixing the slide yet. It is about training your eye to see problems clearly before you start making changes.

The prompts page for this module includes exercises tied to specific slides in the before deck. Work through them in order. By the end you should be able to name the design, content, and structure problem on every slide before AI tells you.

## The Nusantara Capital Scenario

Throughout this workshop you are working on a real problem: your department's quarterly review deck needs to be improved before it goes to senior leadership at Nusantara Capital.

The deck exists. It has real content. The job is not to start over, it is to make what is there work better. That is the most common presentation challenge in any organisation, and it is exactly what this workshop trains you for.

Each module moves this project one step forward. By Module 08 you will have a deck that is cleaner, clearer, and ready to present.

## Discussion: What Makes a Presentation Fail?

Before moving on, think about a recent presentation you sat through, either one you gave or one you attended.

- What was the biggest problem with it?
- Was the issue the design, the content, or the structure?
- At what point did you or the audience disengage?

You do not need to share this, but having a specific example in mind will make the rest of the workshop more useful.
