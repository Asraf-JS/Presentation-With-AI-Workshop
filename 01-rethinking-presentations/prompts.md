# 01 - Rethinking Presentations: Prompts

This page has two parts. Part 1 is tied to the Nusantara Capital before deck and walks you through the workshop exercise. Part 2 is a set of prompts you can take away and use on any deck after the workshop.

Use these in **ChatGPT, Copilot Chat, or any AI chat tool**.

---

## Part 1 - Workshop Exercise

Open the before deck before you start: [Download NC_Q3_2025_Before.pptx](./NC_Q3_2025_Before.pptx)

Work through the slides in order as you go through these prompts.

### Slide 1 - Cover slide

```
Here is a description of a corporate presentation cover slide. Title: "Q3 2025 Departmental Review". The title uses a large bold serif font. The subtitle is a 3-line paragraph explaining what the presentation covers, who prepared it, and when. The date is in a small italic font at the bottom left. The company name is in a spaced-out all-caps font at the top. Identify every design problem on this slide and explain why each one is a problem.
```

### Slide 2 - Agenda

```
A corporate presentation has an agenda slide as its second slide. It lists 9 topics in a numbered list. The audience is senior leadership at a Malaysian investment holding company who already know what the presentation is about. Is this slide necessary? What should replace it, if anything?
```

### Slide 3 - Department Overview

```
Here is the content of a slide titled "Department Overview". It has 8 bullet points, each 2 to 3 sentences long, covering: when the department was founded, what it is responsible for, how many projects it manages, headcount, office locations, stakeholder engagement activities, budget, and training activities. What are the problems with this slide? What is the maximum number of bullet points a content slide should have, and why?
```

### Slide 4 - Q3 Revenue Performance

```
A slide titled "Q3 Revenue Performance" shows a pie chart with 7 segments representing revenue by business unit. The total revenue is RM 4.2M. What is wrong with using a pie chart here? What chart type would communicate this data more clearly, and why?
```

### Slide 5 - Project Status

```
A project status slide contains a table with 10 rows and 6 columns: Project Name, Owner, Start Date, Planned End Date, Status, and Key Issues. The font size is 9pt to fit everything on one slide. Two projects are delayed, one is at risk, and seven are on track. What are the problems with presenting project status this way to senior leadership? How would you simplify this slide to make it more useful for decision-makers?
```

### Slides 6 and 7 - Achievements and Headcount

```
Slide 6 is titled "Key Achievements" and contains these bullet points: revenue increased by 12% this quarter, 3 new clients onboarded, stakeholder satisfaction scores improved, two staff completed training, cost savings of RM 180K achieved, a significant deliverable was completed ahead of schedule. What is missing from each of these bullet points that would make them more meaningful?

Slide 7 is titled "Headcount and Staffing" and contains two separate sections: a headcount table on the left and a staff engagement survey summary on the right. What is the structural problem with this slide?
```

### Slide 8 - Budget Utilisation

```
A budget slide shows a clustered bar chart comparing actual spend versus target across 8 categories: Salaries, Consultancy, Travel, Technology, Training, Office, Marketing, and Misc. Every bar has a data label showing the exact value. There are gridlines on both axes and a grey background on the chart area. List every visual problem with this chart and suggest how to fix each one.
```

### Slides 9 to 11 - Risks, Priorities, and Decisions

```
Slide 9 is a risk table with 4 risks, likelihood and impact ratings, and a status column. Each status says either "Being monitored", "Under review", or "Tracking". The slide ends with this line: "The department continues to track all identified risks and will provide updates at the next leadership review." What is the problem with this closing line? What should it say instead?

Slide 10 lists 7 Q4 priorities as bullet points, each one a full sentence. What visual format would communicate these priorities more effectively than a bullet list?

Slide 11 is titled "Key Decisions Needed" and contains 6 bullet points written as full paragraphs. The actual budget reallocation request that requires senior approval is in bullet point 4. What is wrong with this structure, and how should the slide be redesigned?
```

### Slide 12 - Closing slide

```
The final slide of a corporate presentation shows the text "Thank You" in large bold serif font, centred on a dark navy background. Below it: "For questions, please contact the Strategy and Planning department." There is no contact information, no next step, and no call to action. What is wrong with ending a presentation this way, and what should a closing slide do instead?
```

### Full deck diagnosis

```
I have reviewed a 12-slide corporate quarterly review deck. Here is a summary of what each slide contains:

Slide 1: Cover with inconsistent fonts and wall-of-text subtitle
Slide 2: Agenda listing 9 topics
Slide 3: Department overview with 8 long bullet points
Slide 4: Pie chart with 7 segments showing revenue by business unit
Slide 5: Project status table with 10 rows and 6 columns at 9pt font
Slide 6: Key achievements with vague bullet points and no context
Slide 7: Headcount table and staff engagement survey on the same slide
Slide 8: Budget bar chart with every bar labelled and gridlines on both axes
Slide 9: Risk table with no recommendations
Slide 10: Q4 priorities as a 7-item bullet list
Slide 11: Key decisions buried in paragraph 4 of 6 bullet points
Slide 12: Thank You slide with no call to action

Rate this deck out of 10 on: message clarity, slide design, data communication, and narrative structure. For each category give a score and one sentence explaining it.
```

## Part 2 - Try It Yourself

Use these prompts on any deck you are working on outside the workshop.

### Diagnosing your own deck

```
I have a corporate PowerPoint deck I need to improve. Here is a summary of what it covers: [describe your deck in 2 to 3 sentences]. What are the most likely problems with a deck like this, and what should I fix first?
```

```
Here is a slide from my deck. Title: [paste your title]. Content: [paste your bullet points or describe the visual]. What is wrong with this slide and how would you improve it?
```

### Understanding good design practices

```
What are the 5 most important design principles for a corporate presentation slide? For each one, give a concrete example of what it looks like when the principle is followed and when it is broken.
```

```
I want to improve the visual quality of my presentation without hiring a designer. What are the highest-impact changes I can make to a basic PowerPoint deck using only built-in tools and free resources?
```

### Understanding where AI helps

```
I am preparing a department presentation for senior leadership. Which parts of the presentation process should I use AI for, and which parts should I handle myself? Be specific about what AI is good at and where it falls short.
```

```
What are the limitations of using AI tools like PowerPoint Designer or Canva's AI features for corporate presentations? What should I be careful about?
```

### Framing the improvement project

```
I am about to improve an existing presentation deck rather than build one from scratch. Give me a simple checklist I should work through before I start making changes, covering message, structure, content, and design.
```

```
What is the difference between a presentation that informs and a presentation that persuades? Give me 3 concrete examples of how the same content would be structured differently depending on the goal.
```
