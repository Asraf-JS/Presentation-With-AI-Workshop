# 07 — AI-Assisted Slide Review and Refinement

Before you present, you need to review the full deck as a critic, not as the person who built it. AI can help with this — but only if you give it the right brief.

> **Prompts to Try:** Open the [copy-paste prompt exercises](./prompts) for this module.

## Using AI to Critique Slide Clarity and Flow

The problem with reviewing your own work is that you know what you meant to say. You fill in the gaps automatically. AI does not — which makes it useful for catching problems you have stopped seeing.

### What to ask AI to look at

**Clarity of the key message.** Paste your slide titles in order and ask: "Based on these titles alone, can you tell what the single key message of this presentation is?" If the answer is no, the deck's narrative is buried.

**Logic of the flow.** Ask: "Do these slides tell a coherent story? Where does the logic break down or where do you need to make a jump to follow it?" This surfaces gaps you have glossed over.

**Slide-level clarity.** For individual slides, paste the title and bullet content and ask: "What is the point of this slide? Is it clear from the title alone?"

### What AI cannot do

It cannot tell you whether your content is strategically right. It cannot read the room. It does not know that your CFO already agreed to the budget reallocation in a prior meeting, which changes how you should frame the request. These judgments stay with you.

## Checking for Overloading and Redundancy

### Overloaded slides

A slide is overloaded when it is trying to make more than one point. Signs:
- More than 5 bullet points
- A chart with more than one main message
- A title that covers two different ideas joined by "and"
- Speaker notes that are longer than 200 words because there is too much to explain

**Fix:** Split the slide in two, or cut until only one point remains.

### Redundant slides

Redundancy happens when you have two slides covering the same ground — often because the deck was assembled over time from different sources, or because the same information appears in both a summary and a detail view.

**Fix:** Read every slide title out loud in sequence. If you notice you are making the same point twice, cut one. If both slides are needed, make sure they are clearly serving different purposes.

### The agenda slide problem

Agenda slides at the start of a presentation are almost always redundant in a corporate context. If the meeting was set up properly, the audience already knows why they are there. An agenda slide that lists six topics tells the audience nothing they will remember by slide three.

Exception: If the presentation covers genuinely distinct sections that require different engagement from the audience (e.g., one section needs a decision, one is purely informational), an agenda slide can signal the shifts. Otherwise, cut it.

## Refining Slide Headlines and Visuals

### Passive and vague titles

By this stage in the workshop, most of your slide titles should already state a point. But go through them one more time looking for these patterns:

**Passive titles** state what is being shown, not what it means.
- "Revenue performance" → "Revenue exceeded target for the second quarter running"

**Hedge titles** avoid committing to a position.
- "Some positive Q3 results" → "Q3 closed above target — here is what drove it"

**Question titles** can work, but only if the slide answers the question clearly and directly.
- "Can we maintain momentum in Q4?" only works if the slide says yes or no, not if it just raises more questions.

### Misaligned visuals

Every visual on a slide should serve the headline. Ask for each image, icon, or chart: does this directly support what the title says? If you cannot answer yes immediately, the visual is decorative at best and distracting at worst.

A slide that says "Revenue exceeded target" and shows a bar chart proving it is aligned. The same slide with a stock photo of a handshake is not — the photo adds a vague sense of success without supporting the specific claim.

---

## Final Review Checklist

Before you call the deck done:

- Every slide title makes a point, not just a label
- No slide has more than 5 bullet points
- Every chart has a point-driven title and labels only what matters
- Canva and PowerPoint slides look visually consistent
- The narrative arc is readable from the slide titles alone
- Speaker notes exist for the 5-6 most important slides
- You have read through the deck in slideshow view at full screen
